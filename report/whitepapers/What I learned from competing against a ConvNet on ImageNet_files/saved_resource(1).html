                          Hi Jeff, thank you! 3.5% is quite incredible. I do think the image recognition in ILSVRC is near dead. I wouldn't be surprised about a few extra points, but we're nearing saturation. At the same time, I think I predicted a saturation of ~90% accuracy, and we're now at 95%.                               Janez Pers karpathy 4 years ago

                              Well, I don't think the dataset is solved (or "dead"). First of all, top-5 rule is very forgiving, allowing algorithms to score well with wrong order of submitted categories, and possibly punishing those who get it almost right, but don't get the exact right category into first five (consider for example classes like "tiger cat" and "tabby cat" from ILSVRC 2015). So perhaps rules should be tightened for 2016, and we'll see where state-of-the art actually lies with top-1 rule? There really is no point in including incredibly fine-grained categories, like two kinds of cats that are almost the same, and then allowing such vague classification that is implied in top-5 rule.

                                        First, FYI, your paper link ("ILSVRC paper") is broken; for the convenience of others, it should point to http://arxiv.org/abs/1409.0575.

                                        Thanks very much for this work, writeup, and the tool: this is an amazing statement about the accuracy of computer vision.

                                        I have some questions, possibly nits to pick: in looking at images GoogleLeNet had gotten wrong (with your tool), I found that a lot of them I thought were ground-truth errors, far more than the fraction you quote above. This may be because I am including images where the "correct" classification appears in the image but (in my opinion) it cannot be reasonably claimed to be the focus of the image.

                                        Some examples:

                                        http://cs.stanford.edu/peop... is classified as "hay": "sorrel" is a better answer.

                                        http://cs.stanford.edu/peop... is classified as a "car wheel": I think "pickup truck" is more accurate

                                            karpathy Mod Tim Dierks 6 years ago edited

                                            Thank you, fixed the link!

                                            There are practical challenges with collecting dataset of 1.2 million images. In this case, the image of "hay" for example comes up when you search "hay" in some search engine, indicating that someone probably talked about "hay" in that image, which is why it appears labeled as such. In the binary noise-filtering AMT task, we simply asked the turkers: "Is there hay in this image?", and the answer in this case is yes, which is why it made it through. We're aware of this issue, which is why we allow up to 5 guesses. In my analysis, I would put these types of mistakes under the error of "multiple objects", not under wrong label category.

                                            And you're also right about the one-sidedness of seeing only the ConvNet errors, and being possibly mislead. Many of the images you could probably do much better on, but there is an almost equally-sized portion of images that you would not classify correctly and the ConvNet would.

                                                  karpathy Mod Phil Marshall 6 years ago

                                                  Phil: The ideal task is indeed a detection task where you localize and detect as many objects as there are in an image. But it's still useful to have classification because it's in some sense the simplest Computer Vision task (common denominator, as I like to call it). In detection there are usually many bells and whistles and moving parts. The takeaways can be more complicated.

                                                  Unfortunately I didn't keep a very good track of Hit@1 because we were only going to report the Hit@5 (to be consistent across the paper), so I got sloppy with the ordering over time. In some images I felt I had a clear advantage for Hit@1 if they had a single out of place, salient object (for example a screwdriver that some woman in a suit was holding in her hand - it seems out of place and odd). In other cases, the ConvNet would edge out on Hit@1, especially in fine-grained it was usually better with Hit@1.

                                                  So, hard to say. In retrospect I probably should have spent the extra time to worry about the order.

                                                                                      Bob Sturm 6 years ago

                                                                                      This is a very interesting read! Thank you for doing this and sharing it. However, I don't think your conclusions about the causes of GoogLeNet errors are valid. For instance, you say 6% of its errors are due to "objects of interest in an abstract form". You are claiming a cause of the error. Presumably this number comes by you looking at all the misclassified images and asking what they have in common -- clustering by you -- and then counting the numbers in each cluster. Since you did the clustering according to your own schema, and since there are many more independent variables in an image than "abstract form", you cannot validly claim that this variable is what caused GoogLeNet to fail. A different experiment is needed to make such a claim. Many similar invalid claims have been made in automatic music style recognition. For more on this, see my recent article: http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/....

                                                                                                          Christian Szegedy Tom Dietterich 6 years ago

                                                                                                          I am not concerned about overfitting to the test set too much: during the competition, when you submit your posting, the evaluation server did not return the accuracy, so you could not tweak your algorithm based on the test-set accuracy numbers.

                                                                                                          The reason for people overfitting to MNIST is that the whole test set is released with labels.

                                                                                                          I think it is still possible to to get numbers (1 per week) on the test-set performance, for publication purposes on ILSVRC12 test set, but if somebody tweaks his algorithms or hyperparameters based on those numbers, he will have a hard time (not many measurement points), also it would mean he has some serious integrity issues to start with.

                                                                                                          The (even more outrageous) other possibility, as you mentioned, is that somebody would label the test set.
